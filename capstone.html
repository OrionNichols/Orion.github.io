Robotic Inchworm Pruner (RIP-bot) Capstone Project

Developing a safe and efficient robotic system for pruning coastal redwood trees.

Capstone Gallery

Cutting Force Modeling

One of the first stages of development was determining the force required to cut live Coast Redwood branches. I first developed a theoretical cutting model based on the shear strength of green redwood. While this provided an initial estimate for actuator sizing, it did not account for blade geometry.

To improve the prediction, I developed a cutting model using Merchant Circle Theory that incorporated blade rake angle, friction, and shear angle. This allowed the team to evaluate how blade geometry and material properties influenced cutting forces.

To validate the analytical model, I designed and conducted a guillotine-style cutting experiment capable of producing consistent cuts on live branches. Testing showed that measured cutting forces were substantially higher than the original shear model predicted due to wood anisotropy, blade friction, and experimental pinching effects. These conservative results were used to size the robot's cutting actuator, leading to the selection of a system capable of producing approximately 7 kN of force for branches up to 38 mm in diameter.

Experimental Validation

The first experiment that we conducted was to validate and verify the forces required to cut Coast Redwood branches. In addition to measuring cutting force, we wanted to determine whether the straight-blade cutting process would produce cuts with a quality similar to the bypass loppers currently used in the field.

Fresh Coast Redwood branches ranging from 10-38 mm in diameter were cut while recording force-displacement data using a Shimadzu universal testing machine. Peak cutting force increased with branch diameter, reaching approximately 7.8 kN for the largest branch tested. These experimental results became the basis for actuator selection and the structural loading used throughout the robot design.

Beyond force testing, I evaluated cut quality using a Keyence VR-6000 3D microscope, comparing the robot's cuts against commercial bypass loppers. We measured the surface roughness (peaks per micrometer) and compared those averages with the lopper cuts. Statistical analysis using Mathematica showed no significant difference in surface roughness (p = 0.95), demonstrating that the straight-blade mechanism produced cut quality that was not statistically different from the loppers. This provided the basis upon which our inchworm cutting mechanism was designed.

Structural Analysis and Frame Design

One of my primary responsibilities on the RIP-bot project was verifying that the robot frame could safely withstand the loads generated during cutting and climbing. Since the straight blade cutter required forces up to approximately 7.8 kN, the frame and clamping system needed to resist large bending loads without yielding.

To simplify the analysis, I broke the welded clamp frame into individual structural members that could be modeled as beams in bending. I focused on the actuator support bar, main structural crossbar, clamping feet, bolts, and welds because these were the most likely failure points in the design.

The highest calculated frame stress occurred in the main crossbar at approximately 130 MPa, which remained below the 276 MPa yield strength of the 6061-T6 aluminum frame. The bolts, welds, actuator supports, and clamping feet were also found to remain below their respective yield strengths.

This analysis helped validate the frame before fabrication and showed that the prototype was structurally robust enough for testing. It also identified future opportunities to reduce weight while maintaining a safe factor of strength for the next design iteration.
