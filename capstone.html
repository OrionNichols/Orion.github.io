Robotic Inchworm Pruner (RIP-bot) Capstone Project

Developing a safe and efficient robotic system for pruning coastal redwood trees.

Capstone Gallery

Cutting Force Modeling

One of the largest design challenges was determining the force required to prune live Coast Redwood branches.

I first developed a theoretical cutting model based on the shear strength of green redwood branches. This model established the expected cutting loads and allowed the team to size actuators before hardware was purchased. Experimental testing later showed that the simple shear model underestimated the true cutting force because it did not account for blade geometry or the anisotropic structure of wood.

Using the validated results, the team selected actuators capable of producing approximately 7 kN of force, allowing the robot to reliably cut branches up to 38 mm in diameter.

Experimental Testing

To validate the analytical model, I designed and conducted cutting experiments using a Shimadzu Universal Testing Machine.

Fresh Coastal Redwood branches ranging from 10-38 mm in diameter were cut while recording force-displacement data. Peak cutting force increased with branch diameter, reaching approximately 7.8 kN for the largest branch tested. The experimental results became the basis for actuator selection and structural loading throughout the robot design.

Beyond force testing, I also evaluated cut quality using a Keyence VR-6000 3D microscope, comparing the robot's cuts against commercial bypass loppers. Statistical analysis showed no significant difference in surface roughness (p = 0.95), demonstrating that the straight-blade mechanism maintained cut quality while simplifying the overall robot design.

Structural Design

Once cutting loads were experimentally verified, I used those results to evaluate the structural integrity of the robot's clamping mechanism.

I modeled the primary structural members—including the actuator support bar, main crossbar, clamping foot, welds, and bolted joints—to ensure all components remained below the yield strength of their materials under worst-case cutting loads.

The highest calculated bending stress occurred in the main crossbar at approximately 130 MPa, well below the 276 MPa yield strength of 6061-T6 aluminum. Bolt and weld stresses were also verified to remain within allowable limits, providing confidence that the robot could safely withstand repeated pruning operations.
